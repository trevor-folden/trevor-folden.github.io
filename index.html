Projects

Coffee Grinder

In a group with three other students, we competed in a coffee grinder design and fabrication competition (we won). The project itself was open-ended with the only requirements being the use of the provided bevel gears and burr and grinder. We used a series of methods including manual turning on a lathe, CNC machining, and 3d printing. The system was designed in SolidWorks complimented by a full technical writeup accompanied by individual part drawings.

Pin Tumbler Lock

One of my hobbies is lockpicking so when I was assigned the task of reverse engineering an assembly with at least five parts I decided that a 30+ part traditional pin and tumbler lock made the most sense. I meticulously disassembled and measured each feature with calipers to bring it to life in SolidWorks. I chose to model it after a practice lock for novice lock pickers so that someone unfamiliar with locks can easily see the internals. SolidWorks FEA was used to simulate common types of brute force attacks used on locks (Eg. bolt cutters or shearing the locking lugs).

Hoist Winch

As part of the manufacturing processes class, I was tasked with the fabrication of a hoist winch. The hoist winch was selected due to the wide variety of manufacturing methods required for the multitude of parts. The methods include but are not limited to: casting, manual mill, bending, acetylene torch, hydraulic punch, manual lathe, and CNC. One particularly challenging aspect was that I was forced to cut threads manually on the lathe as opposed to a tap/die set. After cutting one set of threads we walked over and ran the CNC to do the other side of threads much quicker and cleaner. When completed the hoist winch is certified for a 1.0 kip vertical load or 1.5 kip horizontal load.

Iris Mechanism

This iris mechanism is a small project I did in my free time outside of classes. The mechanism itself is my own design, I've seen people use similar mechanisms for novelty ring boxes. I decided to try and design my own take on the mechanism because of how alluring the movement was. I sketched it, designed it in SolidWorks, and once it cools down (it's 115 in Chico over the summer) 3d print it. Eventually, I would like to integrate my design into something practical and fabricate the mechanism out of high-quality materials as opposed to the cheapest PLA money can buy.

Trebuchet

The trebuchet was a fever dream that I undertook with a few of my friends. I was the lead “engineer” on the project. We bought some supplies and used my dad's tools to construct this 15-foot wooden monstrosity. We built it in my driveway and proceed to carry it about a mile to a local middle school in the dead of night. The first two launches were moderately successful hurtling a tennis ball which I have to assume was traveling over Mach 3 over half the length of a soccer field. Tragically on the third launch, the trebuchet realized that it was too powerful for our world and self-destructed. The lesson here is one of planning, though I must note a well-planned, well-executed trebuchet would have taken much longer and been substantially less fun for my friends who are not engineering inclined.
